----------------------------
Suggested base papers
----------------------------

Efthymios Kouloumpis, Theresa Wilson, and Johanna Moore. Twitter sentiment analysis: The
good the bad and the omg! In Lada A. Adamic, Ricardo A. Baeza-Yates, and Scott Counts,
editors, ICWSM. The AAAI Press.

	---> describing different data sets, selection of features for a specific example

Bo Pang and Lillian Lee. Opinion mining and sentiment analysis. Found. Trends Inf. Retr.,
2(1-2):1{135, January 2008.

	---> Basics - why is SA required, typical problems/challenges


Qiang Ye, Ziqiong Zhang, and Rob Law. Sentiment classification of online reviews to travel
destinations by supervised machine learning approaches. Expert Syst. Appl., 36(3):6527{6535,
April 2009.

	---> Explains Naives Bayes, SVM, N-gram based character language model and compares results for a specific example



----------------------------
Other literature
----------------------------

Liu, Bing. "Sentiment analysis and subjectivity." Handbook of natural language processing 2 (2010): 568.
	---> describes the basics of SA very detailed and deals with the problems, different approaches


Ting, S. L., W. H. Ip, and Albert HC Tsang. "Is Naïve Bayes a Good Classifier for Document Classification?." International Journal of Software Engineering and Its Applications 5.3 (2011): 37-46.
	---> compares different machine learning algorithms for SA, explains preprocessing phases/feature selection


Bollen, Johan, Huina Mao, and Alberto Pepe. "Modeling public mood and emotion: Twitter sentiment and socio-economic phenomena." ICWSM. 2011.
	---> a twitter sentiment analysis of specific events between august 1 and december 20, 2008


Pak, Alexander, and Patrick Paroubek. "Twitter as a Corpus for Sentiment Analysis and Opinion Mining." LREC. 2010.
	---> describes how to create a corpus


Agarwal, Apoorv, et al. "Sentiment analysis of twitter data." Proceedings of the Workshop on Languages in Social Media. Association for Computational Linguistics, 2011.
	---> contains a description of twitter data and the respective consequences (emoticons, hashtags, targets, acronyms, spelling mistakes)
	strategies for pre-processing; compares different models


Go, Alec, Richa Bhayani, and Lei Huang. "Twitter sentiment classification using distant supervision." CS224N Project Report, Stanford (2009): 1-12.
	---> characteristics of tweets; approach to cut off emoticons; repeated letters (more than 2) are replaced


Read, Jonathon. "Using emoticons to reduce dependency in machine learning techniques for sentiment classification." Proceedings of the ACL Student Research Workshop. Association for Computational Linguistics, 2005.
	---> topic-dependent, domain-dependent and temporally-dependent meaning of words
	using emoticons
	noisy data
